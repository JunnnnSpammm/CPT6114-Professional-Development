## Answer the following questions:

1.	What are the most effective strategies an individual can use to protect against computer crimes?

Don’t share your personal banking details, don’t download applications outside of smartphone’s official applications platforms, act 
immediately if you suspected you have downloaded a malicious malware.

2.	Discuss the impact of computer crimes on businesses.

Financial loss, disruption in operational, reputational damage, loss of customer trust, theft of sensitive data such as customer private 
information.

3.	What are the possible triggering factors to the rise in numbers of cybercrime cases? 

Rapid technological advancements, many people started using online services without the mindset to protect themselves online, and the low 
risk of prosecution for criminals.

4.	How can individuals recognize and respond to phishing attacks?

Never respond to emails, websites or phone inquiries that request you to provide your credit card, debit card information or account details. 
Never provide your credit or debit card information on a website that is not a secure "https:" site.

5.	How does social engineering contribute to computer crimes, and what strategies can individuals use to defend against social engineering
attacks?

Social engineering exploit human psychology to trick people into revealing sensitive data or granting system access, bypassing technical 
defenses. Individuals can defend themselves by verifying requests through different channels such as calling back official numbers, staying 
skeptical of urgent and unusual offers, using strong multi-factor authentication, avoiding suspicious links or attachments, and questioning 
unexpected physical access requests.

Example Scenario 1: In 2022, Malaysia experienced multiple high-profile data breaches, including incidents involving Maybank (Malaysia’s 
largest bank), the Election Commission (EC), and satellite broadcaster Astro. Personal data of millions of Malaysians were allegedly leaked 
and sold on the dark web, raising concerns about cybersecurity and data protection in the country.

### Questions:

1. What happened?

- Provide a summary of the incident(s), including the nature of the data leak, how it occurred, and the parties affected.

2. Assess the impact of the cyberattack:

Immediate impacts:
- How did the breach affect customers (e.g., fraud, identity theft)?
- What were the operational disruptions for the organizations?

Long-term impacts:
- Financial losses (e.g., regulatory fines, compensation claims).
- Reputational damage and loss of customer trust.
- Legal and regulatory consequences (e.g., investigations under PDPA 2010).

3. Propose strategies for preventing and responding to cyberattacks.

On Dec 30, 13 million account holders from Malaysia’s largest bank Maybank, the EC and satellite broadcaster Astro were alleged to have leaks.
The primary parties affected would have been millions of Malaysian citizens who are customers or voters of the respective organizations. Data 
allegedly leaked included full name, date of birth, IC number, house address, personal contact information, bank account number. These breaches
were not confirmed by Maybank, Astro or the EC. All of them issue statements denying the claims of the data leaks.

The widespread allegation has cause public fear of identity theft and financial fraud. The leaked data containing customers' sensitive 
information could be used by criminals to create highly convincing phishing calls, SMS, or emails to deceive victims into giving up bank 
passwords or OTPs. These allegations had also cause operational disruptions for the organizations as they had to immediately allocate 
significant internal resources to conduct thorough internal audits and forensic investigations to determine the authenticity of the data and 
verify their systems' integrity.

The JPDP was mandated to investigate Maybank and Astro under the Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (Act 709). Since the official investigation 
confirmed that the alleged Maybank and Astro data did not match their actual customer databases and thus no breach was confirmed, neither 
organization faced major regulatory fines, or compensation claims directly under the PDPA 2010. The incident contributed to the overall 
negative public perception regarding data security in Malaysia, leading to increased scrutiny of the organizations' cybersecurity postures, 
regardless of the incident's confirmed authenticity.

Business need to enforce MFA for all user accounts. Regularly audit need to be conducted to remove old, unused, or unnecessary user accounts 
and revoke access permissions for former employees or contractors. Regularly apply security updates and patches to all IT infrastructure such 
as computer, applications and network devices. Always encrypt sensitive data in transit using https and in storage so that the data is 
unintelligible if stolen.
