### Case Study 1

Answer the following questions based on the following excerpt from a posting related to workplace monitoring.

PETALING JAYA: The Bar Council will study if there is a need for new legislation to purchase video and audio surveillance devices in the 
country, says its chairman Lim Chee Wee. He added that although there is no legislation which requires a purchaser to obtain any licence to 
buy surveillance equipment for personal use, a private agency company would require a licence under Section 2(a) of the Private Agencies Act 
1971. “The same applies to Britain and Australia but in these jurisdictions, there is legislation regulating the use of such equipment,” he said.

Lim cited the Australian Listening Devices Act 1984, which prohibits the use of listening devices to record private conversations, without 
the use of a warrant granted by a judge. “Under this act, the piece of equipment used to conduct surveillance must fall within a category 
defined in the legislation. These devices include any instrument, apparatus, equipment or device capable of being used to record or listen 
to a private conversation simultaneously,” he said, adding that the Act also states that a listening device may also have a visual or 
tracking capacity.

He said Australia also had the Workplace Video Surveillance Act 1998 which regulates the use of video surveillance in the workplace. “It 
defines video surveillance as surveillance by a closed-circuit television system or other electronic system for visual monitoring of 
activities on the workplace. “It allows video surveillance if the employee has been given prior written notice of the surveillance. The 
surveillance cameras are clearly visible and there are signs notifying people that they may be under surveillance,” he said.

He added that surveillance that does not satisfy these criteria is considered covert video surveillance under the Act and is unlawful unless 
an authorisation has been issued by a magistrate. In the United Kingdom, Lim said the Private Security Industry Act 2001 required a Public 
Space Surveillance (CCTV) licence to be obtained when manned guarding activities are carried out through the use of closed-circuit 
television equipment.

Workplace monitoring should be implemented in financial institution, high-risk industrial sites, retail, warehousing, healthcare facilities 
and public-facing service centers. These monitoring should be done to prevent internal and external theft, monitor the compliance with 
safety protocols, and to protect employees.

2. Can workplace monitoring bring about any good for an employee? Give examples of situations where workplace monitoring is good for an employee.

Monitoring can prevent false accusations, which protect the physical safety of employees in workspace. CCTV can provide evident to proof the 
innocent of the employee. For example, an employee working alone in a late-night convenience store is protected by CCTV, which acts as a 
deterrent for crime and ensures help can be summoned quickly if a dangerous situation arises.

c. How much privacy is reasonable for an employee to expect in the workplace?

Employees have an absolute expectation of privacy in areas such as restrooms and changing rooms. Thet should be informed of the existence

1. What are the concerns of harmful uses of AI that have been raised in the article?

AI being used in person recognition raises concerns about personal privacy and misidentification. Tracking individuals in conflict zones 
create fear that AI could be used to automate or enhance lethal force, leading to "autonomous weapons" or more efficient warfare.

2. What are the suggestions to avoid harmful uses of AI in the workplace?

We can avoid harmful uses of AI in the workplace by prohibiting AI for surveillance that violates internationally accepted norms. We can 
also split the contract across multiple bidders allows for more oversight and prevents a single entity from having total control over 
sensitive data systems and to prevent monopoly.

3. In your opinion, what kind of job environment could turn beneficial uses of AI into harmful uses, and for what cause it?

A highly competitive and high-pressure can turn beneficial AI into harmful AI. If a company prioritizes speed or cost-saving above all 
else, AI meant to help workers might be used to micromanage them or automate them out of a job without a safety net. In HR or recruitment 
environments, an AI meant to streamline hiring can become harmful if it learns and replicates human biases, leading to systemic discrimination.

4. In your opinion, how should an employee in the workplace act if a company could turn beneficial uses of AI into harmful uses?

The employee should organize with each other to voice ethical concerns to leadership can force a company to reconsider its direction, they 
should refer to codes of ethics in IEEE or ACM to justify their stance. If the company’s direction fundamentally violates the employee’s 
moral compass and internal efforts fail, they should whistle blow and resign.
