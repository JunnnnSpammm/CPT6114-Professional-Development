## Answer the following questions 

1. Why does the first 60 seconds matter in any presentation? How should you start the opening of the first 60 seconds of a presentation? 

The first 60 seconds matter in any presentation because Your audience will decide within 60 seconds if they trust you and decide to engage 
with the rest of your presentation. You should start the opening of the first 60 seconds of a presentation with the following 3 simple 
steps. 

Key idea in 1 sentence, insights in 3 sentences. State your Key Idea in 1-2 sentences, aim is to make this stick in your audience's 
minds 2-3 sentences for the insights the that inform your key idea insights, what you have learned from this key idea. One single sentence 
that boldly states your objective, how to solve problem.

2. Name 3 things that you want your classmates to remember from your presentation

Three things that you want your classmates to remember from your presentation. It could be the design of the presentation that capture and 
hold the audience attention, the choice of material content, the 'message' that they are using

3. What is the suggested structure of your presentation? How will you begin?

Suggested structure of your presentation: Introduction -> Main points with explanations -> Summary or key message. Example: Statistics about 
dengue

4. What is the TRACT method?
    1. Thank the questioner
    2. Repeat the question for all to hear
    3. Answer the question
    4. Check with the questioner if they are satisfied
    5. Thank them again

5. On a graph, what will affect our choices of color combination for data points and lines? What are the ways to differentiate colors?

Contrast and complement will affect your choice of color combination for data points and lines.

Differentiate colors by using contrasting colors, colors but different chroma intensity and lightness value, white background, bright colour 
mixed with gray

What do you think are the mistakes made by the presenter?

The presenter spends most of the time with his back to the audience. Each slide is crammed with full sentences and tiny bullet points. He 
reads the slides word-for-word. Since the audience can read faster than he can speak, they finish the slide, get bored, and stop listening

What are effective tips for giving a good presentation?

10 slides is the optimal number of slides. You should be able to give your talk in 20 minutes, even if you have an hour. Most presenters use 
tiny text and then read it. Using at least a 30-point font forces you to keep your slides visual and concise, ensuring your audience listens

Compare the content of a good and bad presentation in table form.

| Feature | Bad Presentation (The Mistakes) | Good Presentation (The Successes) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Opening** | No formal greeting; jumps straight into the topic without context. | Starts with a warm **"Good morning!"** and clearly states the topic and goals. |
| **Eye Contact** | Stares at the notes/paper; rarely looks at the audience. | Constant **eye contact** with the audience, making the talk feel personal. |
| **Vocal Delivery** | Low volume, mumbled, and monotonous. Hard for the back of the room to hear. | **Projected voice**; clear, confident, and varies in tone to keep interest. |
| **Personal Connection** | Purely factual and dry; feels like reading a textbook aloud. | Uses a **personal story** (about working after school) to build empathy and relevance. |
| **Body Language** | Tense and closed-off; looks uncomfortable and rigid. | **Smiles** and uses open gestures; appears relaxed and approachable. |
| **Engagement** | One-way communication; audience feels ignored. | Directly addresses the listeners' needs and provides **actionable takeaways**. |
| **Closing** | Abrupt ending ("Thank you") without a summary or call to action. | Ends with a **positive summary** and a clear conclusion on why the topic matters. |
| **Distractions** | Phone rings in the middle of the speech (lack of preparation). | Professional environment; focus remains entirely on the message. |

## Class Activity 
### 60-seconds Elevator Speech 

1.	Form a group of the maximum of 6 students. 
    - Elect a timekeeper and a record keeper.
    - A timekeeper is in charge of keeping track of time.
    - The record keeper is in charge of keeping track of the marks.
    - The remaining players are speakers
2.	The timekeeper calls the name of the player to begin
3.	The player chooses a piece of paper from the container. He/she reads the topic out loud and then give the topic paper to the time keeper. 
4.	The timekeeper will say: 'You have one minute on XXXX (name of topic on paper) starting from now.' 
5.	The timer keeper then begins timing.
6.	The speaker stands up and delivers a speech on the topic in 60 seconds. His/her goal is to deliver a speech for at least 30 seconds to get marks. If the speaker stalls out, the speaker sits down and the next speaker is chosen. The first-round ends if all the speakers have delivered their speeches.

7.	Repeat for 3 rounds. Take turns to be a timekeeper and a record keeper.
8.	Keep the marks count public. 
9.	The winner is the person with the most points at the end.
